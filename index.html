Abstract

Cricket is often characterized as a batter’s game due to the significant advantages batters receive from advanced training, favourable pitches, and powerful new bats (Gray-nicolls.co.uk, 2024). Bowlers frequently
exert immense effort with little reward, as batters dominate the field. A recent example is the first Test match of England’s tour of Pakistan, where England’s batters amassed a staggering 823 runs for the loss
of seven wickets. This score, the fourth highest in Test cricket history (Shemilt, 2024), showcases the batters’ endurance in the Pakistani heat but highlights the lack of balance between bat and ball, diminishing 
the competitive nature of the game. Understanding the mechanics of swing bowling is crucial in addressing this imbalance and preserving the sport’s dynamic and competitive nature.

Introduction

The best bowlers possess a repertoire of deliveries to challenge batters. These include off cutters, where the bowler rolls their fingers down the side of the ball to slow it down and induce spin off the pitch, and
short balls, which are pitched halfway down the pitch to bounce up towards the batter’s head. At the start of each innings, fast bowlers often aim to swing the ball, creating lateral movement in the air to make batting
more difficult and increase their chances of taking wickets.

What is swing and how does it work?

The most common form of lateral movement is outswing to a right-handed batter. This is created when the seam (the stitched part of the ball that joins two halves of leather encompassing a cork core) is pointed towards first 
slip (a fielding position to the left of the keeper behind the stumps) and the rough side of the ball is on the side away from the batter (Scobie et al., 2019). This setup causes the ball to move away from the batter due to
the turbulent airflow on the rough side, which slows the ball down and pulls it to the left, resulting in ‘outswing’. Bowlers aim to take wickets by finding the outside edge of the batter’s bat as the ball moves away from it. 
This is why you often see three or four slip fielders positioned behind the batter at the start of a Test innings (Cunningham, 2023).

Bowlers can also create lateral movement back into a right-handed batter by flipping the ball, so the seam and rough side are pointed to the right of the wicketkeeper. This causes the ball to come back into the batter, as the
rough side creates more turbulent airflow than the shiny side, slowing the rough side and pulling the ball across the batter (Scobie et al., 2019). This lateral movement is known as ‘inswing’ (see Figure 1a). Bowlers hope to 
either bowl the batter (hit the stumps) or bring LBW (leg before wicket) into play, where the ball hits the batter’s leg and is adjudged by the umpire to have gone on to hit the stumps had it not hit the batter’s leg 
first (Nag, 2022). For left-handed batters, the terms ‘inswing’ and ‘outswing’ are flipped; however, the methods of creating the swing are the same.

Why does swing vary?

As a cricket ball ages and is hit by the bat multiple times, it deteriorates, causing it to misshape and rough up each side. To create one shiny and one rough side (and therefore swing), players ‘shine’ the ball by rubbing the
less rough side up and down their clothing. It has been found that sweat, saliva, and other illegal ball tampering methods such as sandpapering the rough side can be used to improve the swing of the ball (Scobie et al., 2019). 
The most notorious example of illegal ball tampering was Sandpapergate in 2018 by Australia in the third test against South Africa, which resulted sanctions ranging from 12-month bans to never being allowed to take up leadership
positions again for the involved players (Lemon, 2018).

Weather conditions also have a large effect upon the swing of a cricket ball. Cloudier and cooler conditions tend to create the conditions more suited to swing bowling because in sunnier conditions, air rises from the ground as
it is heated, causing turbulent airflow around the ball (Scobie et al., 2019). This means that there is turbulent airflow on both sides of the ball, causing there to no longer be an imbalance in forces and therefore stopping 
swing. However, in cloudy conditions, the air will not rise and create the turbulent airflow, so the ball will swing. Cooler cloudy conditions also retain the moisture in the pitch, which helps retain the shape of the ball for a
longer period, so the ball will swing for longer periods.

The balls used in England and the West Indies are Dukes balls, can swing for up to 80 overs. However, recently, Dukes balls have been going out of shape quickly, preventing swing throughout their intended lifespan and resulting 
in the ball being swapped for one in better condition (Emmerson, 2022). Typically, Kookaburra balls, used everywhere else except India, swing for the opening overs. The duration of swing varies by country; for example, in Pakistan, 
it can be expected to swing for the opening 20-30 overs (Woolmer, 2013). This leads to spin bowlers being used extensively in the ‘Subcontinent’ (Pakistan, India, Sri Lanka, and Bangladesh) due to the drier and dustier pitches in
Asia, which allow spinners to turn the ball more off the surface (Woolmer, 2013).

Faster bowlers are used in the subcontinent often over halfway through the life of a ball when it begins to ‘reverse swing’. This occurs when the ball has one very rough side, and one side kept in better condition by shining it.
To create reverse swing, the seam and rough side are pointed away from the intended direction of swing (see Figure 1b). Unlike conventional swing, which moves towards the rough side, reverse swing moves in the opposite direction 
due to an area of less turbulent air created on the shinier side of the ball (Roy, 2022). This makes it very difficult for batters to play, as they expect the opposite direction of movement. A notable example is Waqar Younis’ 
performance in 1990 against New Zealand in Pakistan, where he used reverse swing to devastating effect, taking 7 wickets for 76 runs in the second Test in Karachi (Mehta, 2018).

Conclusion

In conclusion, while cricket is often seen as a batter’s game due to the numerous advantages batters have, the skill and variety of deliveries that bowlers bring to the game are crucial in maintaining a competitive balance. 
Understanding the mechanics of swing, both conventional and reverse, highlights the intricate strategies bowlers use to counteract the dominance of batters. Despite the challenges posed by modern equipment and pitch conditions, 
the art of bowling remains a vital and fascinating aspect of cricket, essential for preserving the sport’s dynamic and competitive nature.
